# What Is Ripple?

*For anyone — no data background required. If you've ever wondered what Chris actually does all day, this is it.*

---

## Start with a true story

A few weeks ago, this system flagged **773 providers who had been officially banned from the U.S. healthcare system — and who nonetheless appear in pharmaceutical payment records.** Each one is a lead waiting for a human to review; some of the payments may predate the ban. But every single one is a thread worth pulling.

It found them in about five minutes.

A journalist doing that by hand — cross-referencing a government ban list against a payments database, name by name — would lose *weeks*. And they'd probably miss most of them.

That's Ripple. Now let's back up and explain what it actually is, one step at a time.

---

## The core idea (this is the whole thing)

The U.S. government and organizations around the world publish an *enormous* amount of public data. A list of every licensed doctor. A list of every banned doctor. Every federal contract. Every registered cargo ship. Every sanctioned ship. It's all out there, for free.

But here's the catch: **it's all in separate boxes.** Different agencies, different websites, different formats, none of them talking to each other. The doctor-ban list has no idea the pharma-payment list exists.

> **Ripple's whole job is to connect the boxes — and then notice when two of them disagree in a way that looks wrong.**

Think of it like this:

🗺️ **Imagine every dataset is a city on a map.** On its own, a city is just a city. But the moment you draw the *roads between* the cities — "this exact doctor is in the 'banned' city *and* the 'getting paid' city" — you've found something nobody was supposed to see. **Those roads are the entire product.**

The data is free and anyone can download it. The *connections between* the data? Nobody else has built that. That's the valuable part.

---

## How it works, without the jargon

Ripple does four things, in order. Four simple verbs:

1. **🔭 SCOUT** — It crawls the internet and catalogs hundreds of thousands of public datasets, noting what's in each one. (Like walking every aisle of the world's biggest library and writing down what's on every shelf.)

2. **📥 COLLECT** — It picks a dataset and carefully copies it into one organized warehouse — cleaning up the mess as it goes. A human checks the work at six points along the way. Nothing happens automatically without a thumbs-up.

3. **🔗 CONNECT** — This is the magic. It looks across all the cleaned-up data and finds the *same real thing* showing up in two places it shouldn't. The same doctor. The same company. The same ship.

4. **🔍 EXPLORE** — It turns those findings into maps, profiles, and a list of "leads" worth investigating.

That's it. Scout, collect, connect, explore. Everything else is detail.

---

## The clever part: "facts" vs "leads"

This is the bit that makes Ripple trustworthy instead of just a rumor machine, and it's genuinely smart.

When Ripple connects two records, it asks one question: **how sure are we?**

- **If they share an exact ID number** — like a doctor's official federal license number — then it's the *same person*, guaranteed. Ripple calls this a **fact**. It's allowed to stand on its own.

- **If they only share a *name*** — "John Smith" here, "John Smith" there — well, there are a lot of John Smiths. Ripple calls this a **lead**. It is *never* treated as true on its own. A human has to look at it first.

> **Same ID number = a fact. Same name = just a hunch.** Ripple knows the difference, and it refuses to publish a hunch as if it were proof.

That single rule is why this can be real journalism. Nothing gets called "true" until a person confirms it. The machine finds the smoke; a human confirms the fire.

Real results, today:

## Why this matters

Investigative journalism is slow, expensive, and shrinking — newsrooms are gutted, and the kind of patient cross-referencing that catches wrongdoing is exactly the kind of work that's disappearing.

Ripple is an attempt to make **one person as powerful as an entire research desk.** Not by replacing the journalist — the human still makes every call — but by doing the weeks of grunt-work in minutes, so the human can spend their time on the part that actually needs a human: judgment.

The bet underneath it: as more datasets get connected, the system gets *more* valuable, not less — because every new dataset can connect to all the ones already there. The first connection is interesting. The ten-thousandth is a map of how the world actually works that nobody else has.

---

## The honest part (because honesty is the point)

This is built by **one person**, and it's early. It's a real, working engine — not a slideshow — but it's the foundation of something bigger, not the finished tower:

- Most of its findings so far come from one powerful pairing of datasets. The next phase is loading *more* data so it can find *more kinds* of stories.
- It deliberately uses **conservative, honest numbers** about itself. In fact, one of its proudest features is that **the system catches itself when it's wrong** — it once flagged that millions of rows it had loaded were actually empty, and corrected its own records. A tool built to expose things that don't add up had better add up itself.

That self-honesty isn't a side note. For something whose entire purpose is to hold *other* people's data accountable, being ruthlessly accountable about its own is the whole credibility game.
