# US Politics — The Dark Frontier (what we don't have yet)

_2026-06-30 · 14-region deep-scout (`wf_64b41aae-3b7`): 14 regions, 111 sources, ranked by value × gettability._

## The shape of it

The dark frontier of US political data splits cleanly into TWO realities, and that split decides everything. ABOVE the county line and inside Congress, the data is steel-keyed to the spine Ripple already owns (bioguide / icpsr / fec_id / EIN) — that's where the quick, transformative wins live. BELOW the federal level (states, precincts, cities, school boards, lobbyists, appointees), there is NO shared person ID and never will be; you connect by geography (FIPS/OCD-ID), by fuzzy name+address matching, or not at all. The 2-3 biggest prizes: (1) MEDSL election results — closes the single biggest hole in the whole platform ("we have the money but not who won"), free, easy, and it ships FEC/ICPSR keys INSIDE the data so it bolts straight onto the FEC+Voteview spine. (2) The communications cluster (tweets, press releases, e-newsletters, floor speeches) — all bioguide-keyed, near-trivial to land, and it builds the "say-vs-do" corpus that turns every vote tally into a story. (3) A cluster of plug-and-play ideology/money spines — DIME/CFscores, Shor-McCarty, Google Political Ads, IRS 527s, CES survey microdata — each of which natively carries a key (ICPSR, FEC, or EIN) and joins with zero fuzzy matching. The recurring tax across the dark half is name-resolution engineering: budget for it as a first-class build, not an afterthought, because it's the moat. The recurring trap is license: FollowTheMoney (NC), ACLED (NC), Ballotpedia/BallotReady (paid/proprietary), OpenSecrets (NC), LegiScan (no-redistribute) — all clash with the commercial ceiling and must stay reference-only.

## Quick wins (easy + valuable)

- MEDSL election results — the surprise that it's EASY: free bulk .tab/CSV, ~a day of work, and the House/Senate files carry FEC + ICPSR keys so 'who won' snaps onto FEC money and DW-NOMINATE with ZERO fuzzy matching. Closes the platform's biggest hole. Land it this week.
- DIME / CFscores v4.0 — openly downloadable, attribution-only, NATIVELY keyed on ICPSR + fec_id. The candidate file is a 1-2 day clean bulk load that gives a second, money-based ideology score to triangulate against DW-NOMINATE. Highest ROI single file in the frontier.
- Google Political Ads (BigQuery) — public dataset, free key, daily, and it carries the advertiser-declared FEC committee ID. STEEL join for 'reported IEs vs. actual ad spend.' GCS->Parquet->Snowflake, ~a week, near-zero matching effort.
- congress-press + congresstweets — bioguide baked into press releases, one crosswalk hop for tweets. MIT-licensed, daily-maintained, near-trivial bulk onboards that complete the say-vs-do corpus.
- CES (Cooperative Election Study) — CC0, no auth, one Dataverse download, ~60k respondents/year already tagged with congressional district + county FIPS + validated vote. The missing 'voice of the electorate' column, joins via geography to named members.
- Open States bulk + openstates/people — public-domain, free, idempotent-friendly (Postgres dump / per-session files). Lands the entire 50-state legislative spine AND the ocd-person->bioguide career-jumper bridge in ~3-4 onboards.
- IRS POFD (527s) + EO BMF + ProPublica Nonprofit API — free, no-auth, EIN-keyed. Four straight onboard.py runs give the disclosed-but-FEC-invisible money layer with a STEEL EIN self-join.
- Statements of Administration Policy (unitedstates archive) — tiny, high-signal, bill_id-keyed dataset that adds the executive veto signal joinable straight to billstatus + roll-call votes. Easy grab inside the legislative-substance region.
- GAO recommendations CSV + FOIA.gov API + OPM PLUM register — three easy agency-keyed CSV/API pulls that drop in as oversight marts joining cleanly to USASpending/SAM by agency.
- Frozen FiveThirtyEight polls archive (GitHub, CC-BY) — snapshot it NOW before it rots; 538 is shut down and the repo will never update again. Finite CSV trove, a pollster-accountability dataset in itself.

## Hard but worth it

- State Campaign Finance — the entity-resolution campaign. Raw data is mostly free and clean per-state (WA/TX easy, CA via the Civic Data Coalition mirror, FL/NY grindy). The real build is a deliberate, scored entity-resolution layer: probabilistic donor matching across states + FEC, plus a hand-curated state-candidate->bioguide crosswalk. Promise a probabilistically-linked money layer with a confidence score, NOT key-perfect federal joins. Highest investigative payoff per dollar precisely because nobody else has it wired to a federal spine.
- The name->bioguide disclosure crosswalk (STOCK Act). Build it ONCE as a reconciliation table and you unlock every insider-trading / conflict-of-interest story against the spine — trade date vs. vote date vs. committee jurisdiction. The crosswalk IS the project; the PDF/OCR transaction parser with a manual-review queue is the supporting grind.
- The witness->entity bridge (legislative substance). Congressional hearing witnesses have no unique ID and inconsistent org capture — connecting 'this witness testified as an independent expert' to LDA lobbying / USASpending contractor (UEI) / IRS nonprofit (EIN) is a probabilistic name+org resolution build. Same class as the NPI<->EIN gap, and the highest-value/hardest piece of the region.
- Bluesky bioguide crosswalk — a moat by construction. No maintained bioguide<->Bluesky map exists; you scrape starter-pack member lists, resolve handles to DIDs, hand-match to bioguide. Bluesky's open AT Protocol firehose makes the ongoing pull cheap (unlike X's locked API). Net-new construction, high uniqueness.
- The EIN<->fec_id probabilistic bridge (dark money). No official crosswalk exists between FEC committees and nonprofit EINs. Build an explicit scored crosswalk (name+address+treasurer match), label it PROBABILISTIC so nothing downstream treats it as STEEL. This is what draws the c4->c4->527->super-PAC laundering PATH end-to-end.
- The judge-attachment build (lower judiciary). The IDB and USSC sentencing files have judge fields deliberately blanked — re-attach judges by matching to RECAP dockets. Hard-but-possible value-add nobody has done at scale; turns district-level disparity into judge-level accountability.
- FCC OPIF political-file extraction — the 'Free the Files' problem nobody has maintained since 2012. API is free and open but the payload is unstructured scanned NAB-form PDFs needing OCR + parsing. Scope it as a deliberate OCR/extraction build starting with rate-card/sponsor data for top federal races only.
- NYC as the local proof-of-pattern. NYC CFB money + eLobbyist are the richest, cleanest big-city files; prove the local->federal name-match pattern (lean on the existing connect/ module) on NYC before scaling to other cities. The acquisition is easy; the name-resolution layer welding local names to the federal spine is the ongoing investment.
- The person-resolution bridge for executive personnel. Build an appointee/person crosswalk (name+agency+locale, seeded by OpenSanctions PLUM entity IDs) so PLUM names match into FEC/FARA/LDA — a few days of fuzzy-matching that turns a pile of rosters into the revolving-door engine.
- Shor-McCarty + advocacy-scorecard name matching. Stitching Shor-McCarty ideology onto OpenStates legislators (name+state+party+chamber) and resolving Vote Smart / advocacy scorecards to bioguide is bounded but per-group manual QA — bad matches silently corrupt every downstream story, so budget real care.

## Cross-cutting themes

- THE NO-KEY-BELOW-FEDERAL WALL is the defining fact of the entire frontier. Above the county line and inside Congress, data hard-keys on bioguide/icpsr/fec_id/EIN. Below it — states, precincts, cities, school boards, lobbyists, appointees, judges, survey respondents — there is NO shared person ID and there never will be (state CF mints per-state filer_ids, OCD-person ids are random UUIDs, ethics offices key on names). Design every registry row honestly: STEEL only for Congress + career-jumpers + EIN self-joins; GEO (FIPS/OCD-ID/lat-lon) for places; PROBABILISTIC (name+address) for everyone else. Never promise a clean local-to-federal entity join.
- NAME-RESOLUTION IS THE MOAT, NOT THE DOWNLOADS. Across at least 8 of the 14 regions the data lands easily and free — the real, recurring cost is the entity-resolution/crosswalk layer that welds dark-half names to the federal spine. This is exactly the 'bridge fuel' the memory flags as starved. Build it as a first-class, reusable artifact (lean on the existing connect/ module: bridge.py, match.py, resolve.py, fingerprint.py), scored and confidence-tagged, and the same machinery pays off across state CF, disclosure, witnesses, dark money, local, and personnel. The crosswalks ARE the product.
- THE PDF / OCR / SCRAPE BURDEN recurs wherever ethics offices or courts built the systems: FCC OPIF (scanned NAB forms), STOCK Act PTRs (transaction tables), OGE 278e (Lotus Notes PDFs), judicial disclosures, committee-report/hearing bodies, gift/travel filings. The metadata is usable on day one; the body text needs a deliberate parsing/NLP/OCR pass with a manual-review queue. Treat text-mining as its own scoped build, separate from the metadata onboard.
- LICENSE LANDMINES threaten the commercial/freelance ceiling and must be triaged before anything ships publicly. Non-commercial: FollowTheMoney (CC BY-NC-SA), OpenSecrets bulk (educational/NC), ACLED (NC), OpenSanctions PLUM (NC), Shor-McCarty/WMP (academic). No-redistribute / proprietary: LegiScan (no derivatives), Ballotpedia & BallotReady (paid, proprietary IDs), Transparency USA, AdImpact, LegiStorm, CapitolTrades. Dave Leip (redistribution contract). Treat ALL of these as internal reference/validation/benchmark sources — build AGAINST them, never ON them.
- THE STEEL SEAM THAT KEEPS APPEARING — three sources embed the federal key INSIDE the data so they bolt on with no bridge: MEDSL (FEC+ICPSR in House/Senate files), DIME (ICPSR+fec_id natively), and Google Political Ads (advertiser-declared FEC committee ID). Plus JCS judicial ideology is literally COMPUTED FROM the DW-NOMINATE Ripple already holds. Prioritize these — a shipped crosswalk is worth more than any data you'd have to entity-resolve yourself.
- GEOGRAPHY IS THE UNIVERSAL BACKSTOP. Where no person ID exists, FIPS / congressional district / OCD-division-id / lat-lon is the reliable weld: county results, precinct polygons (VEST/RDH spatial join), CES district opinion, protest events (CCC FIPS), local money (OCD->FIPS), oversight (agency). Lean on Census relationship/crosswalk files (precinct/VTD->block->CD/SLD) to roll messy sub-county data up to clean district keys. Geographic is the honest grain — embrace it rather than overpromising person-level joins.
- SEVERAL SOURCES ARE FROZEN, SUNSETTING, OR GOING DARK — grab the archives NOW: FiveThirtyEight (shut down, repo frozen), FollowTheMoney (frozen at 2024, sunsetting into OpenSecrets), Stock Watcher repos (single-maintainer staleness), DCinbox (single academic, mirror it), White House visitor logs (current admin releases nothing for 5+ years), FOIAonline (decommissioned 2023). And document the streaming/CTV ad VOID explicitly — absence as a publishable finding.
- SINGLE-MAINTAINER / BUS-FACTOR RISK is concentrated in exactly the high-value community datasets: congresstweets, congress-press, DCinbox, Stock Watcher, OpenElections (volunteer, uneven coverage), Vote Smart (near-closure, stale 2021 API). Mirror them on ingest and don't architect a live pipeline that assumes they keep updating — verify freshness before trusting any as a live pipe.

## Per-region detail

### Election Results & Outcomes (who actually won) — president, Congress, state, local; turnout; ballot measures; redistricting
_The "who won" half of US politics is genuinely dark in Ripple — every outcome source (MEDSL, VEST, EAVS, Ballotpedia) is merely scouted, none landed — and one source (MEDSL House/Senate) carries FEC/ICPSR candidate IDs that bolt election outcomes directly onto Ripple's federal money+votes spine._

- **Gettability:** moderate · **Verdict:** GET IT — this is the highest-leverage dark region for Ripple because it closes the 'money-in but not who-won' gap on data Ripple already half-owns, and the catalog confirms it's wide open (MEDSL, VEST, EAVS, Ballotpedia all merely scouted, zero landed). Sequence by effort-vs-payoff: (1) LAND MEDSL FIRST — free, bulk, easy, .tab/CSV, and it carries the FEC/ICPSR keys that bolt election outcomes onto the existing FEC + Voteview spine. This one source delivers ~80% of the region's investigative value (full president/senate/house results 1976-2024) for maybe a day of work. (2) Add the free turnout layer — EAVS (jurisdiction FIPS), McDonald VEP, Census CPS/CVAP — clean public data, straightforward FIPS/state joins. (3) Add NCSL ballot measures — small clean academic CSV for 1902-2016, scrape current. (4) OpenElections + VEST/RDH for precinct depth — medium effort: 50 inconsistent schemas, free-text precinct names, and RDH's account/identity gate; budget real normalization time and lean on Census relationship files + VEST geometry to roll precincts up to clean district keys. WHAT IT TAKES / watch-outs: the only money/legal friction is at the edges — Ballotpedia (paid + attribution) for LOCAL coverage and Dave Leip (paid + redistribution-license) for deep history; skip both until a specific local/historical story demands them, because MEDSL+EAVS+Census+NCSL cover the spine for free. Be honest in the registry that the congressional join is STEEL but partial (FEC/ICPSR not fully populated — needs a name+state+district fuzzy fallback) and that precinct/local has NO entity key (GEO/spatial join only). Net: low-cost, high-payoff, mostly-free — land MEDSL this week.
- **Join-key reality:** This is the crux and the honest answer is two-tiered. ABOVE the county line it connects to the federal spine; BELOW it, you're on geography and string-matching alone.  THE BRIDGE THAT EXISTS (STEEL): MEDSL's House and Senate returns carry FEC candidate IDs and ICPSR IDs 'where they can' populate them. That is the golden seam — it means a House/Senate WINNER's vote total joins directly to (a) Ripple's FEC money (fec_id → raised, PAC->candidate, IEs, donations), and (b) Voteview/bioguide/DW-NOMINATE via ICPSR. So for Congress, 'who won + how much they raised + how they voted + how extreme they are' becomes ONE keyed table. Caveat: the FEC/ICPSR columns are NOT 100% populated, so expect a fuzzy-match fallback (name + state + district + cycle) for the gaps. Still, this is the single best join in the whole region and it's why MEDSL is the priority.  THE GEO JOIN (GEO tier, reliable but not an entity ID): Everything else keys on FIPS / GEOID / district code. County president results (MEDSL, Leip) join on county FIPS; CVAP and CD-level data join on congressional district; turnout joins on state. These are clean, stable joins to Census geography and to each other — but FIPS connects PLACES, not PEOPLE/MONEY. You can say 'this county's results vs. its federal contract dollars (USASpending) vs. its demographics,' but you cannot key a county result to a person.  THE WALL (PROBABILISTIC / no shared ID — the state/local crux): Precinct results (OpenElections, VEST) have NO stable precinct ID — precinct is a free-text name that differs across files and even across years within a state. Candidate is a free-text name with no FEC ID at state/local level (FEC only regulates federal races). So state-legislative and local 'who won' has zero entity-level key to the federal spine. Two escape hatches: (1) SPATIAL — VEST/RDH precinct polygons let you lat/lon join precinct results to anything geographic, bypassing the missing ID; (2) CENSUS RELATIONSHIP/CROSSWALK FILES — precinct/VTD → block → CD/SLD rollups let you aggregate messy precinct data up to clean district keys. And state legislators themselves only get an entity ID via the already-PLANNED Open States / LegiScan ingest (people_id), NOT via the election data.  Bottom line: Congress connects hard, counties connect by geography, precincts/local connect only by lat/lon or string-matching. Don't promise a clean local-to-federal entity join — promise a geographic spine with a STEEL congressional seam.
- **Connects to:** Wires straight into the existing federal spine and fills its biggest hole. Ripple already holds every member of Congress (bioguide/icpsr), all roll-call votes, DW-NOMINATE, bill status, committee membership, FEC money (fec_id), and USASpending/SAM — i.e. everything about an officeholder EXCEPT how they got the office. MEDSL House/Senate is the missing keystone: its FEC_candidate_id and ICPSR columns let 'who won + by how much' snap onto fec_id (money) and icpsr/bioguide (votes + ideology) with no new bridge to build — the crosswalk is shipped inside the data. Below Congress, it extends the GEO axis Ripple already uses (FIPS joins to USASpending, Census, contracts) so county/precinct results become another FIPS-keyed layer in the same geographic moat. The already-PLANNED Open States / LegiScan ingest supplies the people_id that would later let STATE-legislative winners (from OpenElections/RDH) connect to state bills/votes — so landing election outcomes now sets up that future join. And VEST/RDH precinct geometry gives Ripple a lat/lon spatial-join capability that backstops every place-based connection where a shared ID doesn't exist.

### State Legislatures (all 50 + DC/PR): bills, votes, sponsors, members, sessions, and state-legislator ideology (Shor-McCarty NPAT)
_The 50-state legislative record is fully gettable and mostly free via OpenStates (public domain) + LegiScan (free but redistribution-restricted), and state-legislator ideology lives in one common space with federal DW-NOMINATE thanks to Shor-McCarty NPAT -- but the whole region floats in the Open Civic Data ID universe with NO steel join to the federal bioguide spine, except for the legislators who later went to Congress._

- **Gettability:** easy · **Verdict:** GETTABILITY: easy, and this is one of the highest-leverage frontier regions for Ripple. The two load-bearing sources (OpenStates bulk + openstates/people crosswalk) are free, public domain, and idempotent-friendly (Postgres dump / per-session files with natural hashes) -- a straightforward bulk-CSV/JSON onboard, no auth wall. Shor-McCarty is a small free academic file. LegiScan is free to pull but redistribution-restricted -- treat it as internal-only backfill, not a published source. WHAT IT TAKES: (1) ~3-4 onboards: OpenStates bills/votes/sponsors, OpenStates+people legislators with the ocd-person->bioguide bridge, Shor-McCarty ideology, and optionally LegiScan-as-validation. (2) Build one fuzzy name+state+party+chamber+session matcher -- the ONLY real engineering cost in the region -- to (a) stitch Shor-McCarty ideology onto OpenStates legislators and (b) resolve LegiScan<->OpenStates. Budget real care here; bad matches silently corrupt every downstream story. (3) Accept the join-key truth up front: state<->federal is steel ONLY for career-jumpers (via bioguide port) and GEO otherwise -- design the registry JOIN_KEYS as ocd-person (STRONG within region), bioguide (STEEL but sparse), state_fips/district (GEO). HARD STOP on expecting a universal state<->federal person key -- it does not exist and never will; stop anyone who plans the data model assuming it does. Net: cheap to land, the crosswalk + ideology common-space make it punch far above its cost, and it converts Ripple from a federal platform into a cradle-to-Congress one.
- **Join-key reality:** Brutally honest: the federal spine is bioguide / icpsr / fec_id / UEI. State legislatures live in a DIFFERENT universe -- Open Civic Data ocd-person ids, which are RANDOM UUIDs (uuid1), stable inside OpenStates but not derivable and not shared with anyone else. LegiScan has its own people_id; Shor-McCarty has its own st_id. None of these natively equal a bioguide. So there is NO steel state<->federal person key for the ~99% of state legislators who never go to Congress. TWO real bridges exist, each partial: (1) The STATE->CONGRESS career bridge: openstates/people ports congress-legislators in the same schema, so a legislator who served in a statehouse AND then in Congress can be wired ocd-person -> bioguide. That is a clean, steel join -- but only for the subset who made the jump (high investigative value: pipeline tracking, but a minority of rows). (2) GEO bridge: every state legislator maps to a district -> state_fips / district, which joins to Census, redistricting, and (loosely) to federal House districts geographically -- a GEO-tier key, not a person key. For wiring the three state datasets to EACH OTHER (OpenStates bills <-> Shor-McCarty ideology <-> LegiScan status), the join is name + state + party + chamber + session -- PROBABILISTIC, not steel; the Shor-McCarty authors themselves populated those fields from OpenStates, which is the good news (their fields are designed to line up). Bottom line: within-region joins are tractable name-matching; the cross-region join to the federal spine is real but narrow (career-jumpers) plus geographic.
- **Connects to:** Ripple already holds the entire federal layer hard-keyed on bioguide/icpsr/fec_id, including DW-NOMINATE ideology and all roll calls. This region is the FARM SYSTEM beneath it. The wire-in path: (1) Land OpenStates (public domain, free, idempotent via the monthly Postgres dump / per-session files) as the spine of the state-leg sub-library, keyed on ocd-person. (2) Land openstates/people YAML as the crosswalk table -- this is what carries ocd-person -> bioguide for career-jumpers, the single join that connects state to Ripple's existing federal spine (mirrors exactly the 'bioguide-via-other_identifiers bridge' already noted in memory). (3) Land Shor-McCarty NPAT and match it to OpenStates legislators by name+state+party+chamber, giving every state legislator a number directly comparable to Ripple's existing DW-NOMINATE -- one ideology ruler from statehouse to Senate floor. (4) Use LegiScan as an internal backfill/validation layer for bill status and full text, NOT as a public-facing store (license). The GEO key (state_fips/district) also wires state legislators to Census and redistricting data. Net effect: Ripple stops being a federal-only platform and becomes the only place where a politician's whole arc -- statehouse vote -> state ideology -> jump to Congress -> federal vote -> federal money -- lives under one roof.

### State Campaign Finance (50 state disclosure portals + aggregators: who funds state candidates, parties, PACs, ballot measures)
_The frontier's biggest dollar volume and worst plumbing: ~50 disconnected state disclosure portals, one stalled-out academic aggregator (FollowTheMoney, frozen at 2024 mid-merger), and zero common entity IDs -- huge investigative payoff, but every join is name-and-address fuzzy matching, not a key lookup._

- **Gettability:** moderate · **Verdict:** GET IT -- but stage it and budget for entity resolution. The raw data is overwhelmingly free, public-record, and clean-licensed at the per-state level (CA, TX, WA, FL, NY, IL all downloadable with no/low friction), so legal risk is low EXCEPT the two aggregators: FollowTheMoney is CC BY-NC-SA (noncommercial -- a problem for the ceiling/freelance-commercial goal; treat as reference, license commercially only if it becomes core) and Transparency USA is proprietary (reference only). The work splits into three tiers: (1) EASY WINS -- Socrata states (WA and peers) and clean bulk-ZIP states (TX); ingest these first, they're a weekend each. (2) GRINDY BUT DONE-BEFORE -- CA CAL-ACCESS (use the California Civic Data Coalition cleaned mirror, not raw), FL (scripted query-export), NY (join contribs to committees, or lean on TAP). (3) BACKFILL/SANITY -- pull The Accountability Project CSVs to mirror ~36 states cheaply and validate your own pulls. What it would TAKE: ~1 ingestion pattern per portal type (Socrata API / bulk-ZIP / query-scrape) reused across states -- maybe 4-6 distinct scrapers covers 40+ states; THEN the hard part -- a deliberate entity-resolution/crosswalk layer (probabilistic donor matching + a hand-curated state-candidate->bioguide bridge) because there is no steel key to the federal spine. Don't promise key-perfect federal joins; promise a probabilistically-linked state money layer with a transparent confidence score. Highest investigative payoff per dollar in the whole frontier, precisely because nobody else has it wired to a federal spine.
- **Join-key reality:** This is the brutal truth and the whole reason the region is dark: there is NO shared entity ID anywhere in state campaign finance. Each state mints its own filer_id (WA filer_id, CA FPPC/filer_id, TEC filerIdent, NY filer id) that is meaningless across state lines and carries ZERO link to the federal spine -- no FEC committee ID, no FEC candidate ID, no bioguide, no ICPSR, no EIN, no UEI. State disclosure forms generally do NOT collect EIN for corporate/PAC donors and do NOT collect any federal candidate ID. So you cannot key-join state CF to Ripple's FEC/Congress data; you can only fuzzy-match on (name + employer + occupation + city/state/zip), which is probabilistic, dirty, and error-prone (typos, abbreviations, 'self'/'retired', JR/SR, business DBAs). Three partial bridges exist, all soft: (1) a candidate who runs federally THEN state, or sits in a state legislature THEN Congress, can be name+state matched to bioguide/ICPSR -- a STRONG-ish but still manual crosswalk you'd have to build and curate by hand. (2) A big corporate/individual donor giving both state and federal can be name-matched FTM<->FEC, exactly the entity-resolution problem FTM/OpenSecrets already half-solved with their industry/economic-interest codes -- which you could adopt as a tagging layer. (3) Geocoded lat/lon (FTM provides it; others you'd geocode yourself) gives you GEO-tier joins to district/FIPS, letting you tie money to places even when you can't tie it to people. Bottom line: state CF connects to the federal spine PROBABILISTICALLY, never on a steel key. Plan for an entity-resolution / crosswalk-curation layer as a first-class part of the build, not an afterthought.
- **Connects to:** Wires in as a new family of loc_/st_ sources feeding LIBRARY_RAW.LANDING (one landing table per state portal, e.g. ST_CA_CALACCESS, ST_TX_TEC, ST_WA_PDC), each registered in SOURCE_REGISTRY with JURISDICTION=state and JOIN_KEYS reflecting the hard truth (state filer_id only, no federal key). The real architectural addition is a NEW BRIDGE/ENTITY-RESOLUTION layer in LIBRARY_STAGING/INTERMEDIATE -- exactly the 'bridge fuel' the memory notes say is starved. Two crosswalks to curate: (a) state_candidate -> bioguide/ICPSR (name+state+office, manual-assisted), turning state money into a feeder for the existing Congress/DW-NOMINATE/roll-call spine; (b) donor entity resolution across states + FEC, ideally seeded by adopting FollowTheMoney's industry/economic-interest codes as a tagging vocabulary. Geocoded donations join to the geographic spine (FIPS/district) the Library already leans on. Net: state CF is the missing 'before Congress' and 'parallel to Congress' money layer that makes the federal spine a full influence graph instead of a federal-only one.
- **Unlocks:** Federal-to-state money laundering of influence: a corporate donor or mega-donor who is capped/scrutinized at the FEC level pouring unlimited money into no-limit states (TX, VA) -- Ripple already has the FEC side, state CF completes the picture.; The pipeline before Congress: track who bankrolled a member's STATE-legislature career (via name+state crosswalk to bioguide), then watch their federal votes/sponsorships -- 'who paid for this Senator before they were a Senator.'; Same-donor-everywhere maps: one PAC or family funding aligned candidates across 10+ states simultaneously (e.g. ideological networks, single-issue money) -- impossible to see in any single portal, visible only once you aggregate + entity-resolve.; Ballot-measure money: FTM uniquely covers ballot-measure committees -- follow industry money (gambling, energy, crypto, pharma) fighting state referenda, then cross-reference the same interests' FEC/lobbying/FARA footprint Ripple holds.; State-contractor-to-state-donor loop: pair state CF donors against USASpending federal contractors (name match) -- and, where state procurement data exists, the classic 'pay-to-play' donate-then-win-the-contract story.; Dark-money seams: state PAC-to-PAC transfers that terminate at a federal super PAC, or vice versa -- the transfer chains that only appear when state + federal sit in the same warehouse.; Geographic heat: geocoded donations mapped to legislative districts/FIPS, joined to Census/demographics already adjacent in the Library -- 'which neighborhoods fund which politics.'

State legislators, state lobbyists, and statewide executives carry NO bioguide/icpsr/fec_id/UEI. The internal keys are all source-local namespaces that don't talk to each other: FollowTheMoney Entity ID (EID), Open States person uuid/ocd-id, CA filer_id, TX filer id, NY entity ids, Ballotpedia page ids. There are exactly three real bridges, in descending order of strength: (1) THE PERSON BRIDGE — the Open States 'people' crosswalk carries votesmart + ballotpedia + wikidata for every state legislator/governor, and when one of those people ALSO served in Congress their record gains bioguide/fec — so anyone who climbed the state→federal ladder is hard-joinable to Ripple's existing member spine (this is your steel thread, but it only covers the climbers, a few hundred people, not the ~7,400 sitting state legislators). (2) THE GEOGRAPHY BRIDGE — every state actor sits in a state + district (ocd-id), which joins to FIPS and therefore to Census/USASpending/everything geographic Ripple holds; this is GEO-tier, reliable but coarse. (3) THE ENTITY-NAME BRIDGE — lobbying clients and campaign donors are companies/orgs whose NAMES can be fuzzy-matched (then hand-verified) to EIN/CIK/UEI entities in the federal data; this is PROBABILISTIC, the highest-value-but-dirtiest link, and it's exactly the bridge-fuel problem already in Ripple's memory. So: no free lunch on a join ID. The platform play is to treat each state's world as a self-contained influence graph (donor→state official, client→state lobbyist→bill/vote) that connects to the federal spine through people-who-went-federal, through geography, and through fuzzy entity names — not through a shared person ID.
- **Connects to:** Slots directly into the existing politics domain and the three GAP buckets the repo already named. Open States/LegiScan (st_openstates, st_legiscan are already registered) become the state-person + state-activity spine, mirroring the federal member_spine/roll-call/bill marts — same schema shape, parallel marts (e.g. politics__state_member_spine, politics__state_money_to_vote). FollowTheMoney fills gap_state_campaign_finance AND gap_state_lobbying_disclosure in one free feed for the ~20 disclosing states; per-state bulk (CA/NY/TX/WA) deepens the high-value states. The Open States crosswalk (votesmart/ballotpedia/wikidata, plus bioguide for state→federal climbers) is the join into the existing federal member crosswalk; ocd-id/district→FIPS joins state actors to everything geographic; lobbying-client and donor names feed the same probabilistic entity-name bridge (→EIN/CIK/UEI) the connect/leads layer already wrestles with. NAAG multistate settlements (already a mart) gains its political-money context. Net: it extends Ripple's federal accountability stat-line downward into 50 statehouses without changing the architecture — three layers, same naming, st_ prefix.
- **Unlocks:** State-bill positions vs. donor money: who funded the legislators who killed/passed a bill — fuse Open States/LegiScan roll-call votes with FollowTheMoney contributions to the same legislators, the state version of Ripple's federal money→vote story.; Client-to-lobbyist-to-outcome chains: trace a company's state lobbying registrations (CA/NY/TX/WA bulk) to the specific bills it lobbied and how its funded legislators voted — multi-state, so you can show the SAME corporate client running the SAME playbook across statehouses.; The state→federal escalator: use the Open States→bioguide bridge to follow a politician's money and votes from statehouse to Congress in one continuous timeline — Ripple already owns the federal half, this completes the arc.; Revolving door: cross former state legislators/officials (Open States term-end dates) against later appearance as registered lobbyists in the same state's lobby database — a clean, automatable 'they wrote the rules, now they sell access' query in the ~26 full-disclosure states.; Personal-enrichment conflicts: where a state has usable financial-disclosure data, join an official's disclosed assets/outside income to the industries they lobby-shepherd or contract with — the state analogue of Ripple's House/OGE net-worth work.; Multistate AG coordination money: Ripple already holds the NAAG multistate-settlements mart — wire it to which AGs (NAAG/Open States roster) joined which settlements and who funds those AGs' campaigns (FollowTheMoney tracks AG races), exposing donor influence over which corporations get sued or spared.; Dark-vs-disclosed map as a story in itself: 17 states don't require lobbying compensation disclosure and MT went paper-only after 2019 — the absence is the story; a coverage map of where influence is legally invisible is publishable on its own.; Secretary-of-state election-administration angle: NASS roster + FollowTheMoney SoS-race money shows who funds the officials who run elections, a live accountability beat.

### Local / Municipal Politics (mayors, city councils, county boards, school boards; big-city campaign finance & lobbying; local election results & ballot measures)
_The single darkest, most fragmented region in US political data: ~519k elected officials and thousands of independent campaign-finance/lobbying systems, with NO national local-official key and zero federal-linkable IDs at the local level -- but a dozen big-city ethics agencies publish genuinely clean, bulk-downloadable money/lobbying data, and OCD-IDs + a handful of aggregators give you just enough geographic spine to connect it to FIPS and the federal layer probabilistically._

- **Gettability:** moderate · **Verdict:** GET IT -- but in tiers, and budget for the name-resolution layer, not the downloads. Tier 1 (easy, do now): the open big-city money/lobbying systems are clean bulk CSV / free Socrata APIs with permissive terms -- NYC CFB, NYC eLobbyist, SF (DataSF), Seattle, Philadelphia, plus OCD-IDs and OpenElections. These land in days as loc_ sources; NetFile is one integration that unlocks a swath of California. Tier 2 (medium): LA and Chicago/IL-SBOE need scrape/export and filtering work; OpenElections needs per-state stitching. Tier 3 (commercial decision): Ballotpedia and BallotReady/CivicEngine are the only way to get a NATIONAL officeholder roster for the ~519k local officials -- both are paid, license-restricted, proprietary-ID, and NOT freely redistributable. Use them as an internal bridge/enumeration, not as published raw data, or accept you'll never have full national local-official coverage. The real cost everywhere is NOT acquisition -- it's that nothing carries a federal ID, so the value is unlocked only by the probabilistic name+address matcher you build on top (leaning on the existing connect/ module). What it would take: ~1-2 weeks to land Tier 1 + NetFile and register them keyed on OCD/FIPS+name; then the bigger, ongoing investment is the entity-resolution layer that welds local names to the federal spine. Highest-leverage first move: NYC (CFB money + eLobbyist) as the flagship vertical -- richest disclosure, cleanest files, biggest city -- to prove the local->federal name-match pattern before scaling to other cities.
- **Join-key reality:** Brutal truth: there is NO national local-official key and NO federal-linkable ID anywhere in local politics. Three hard facts. (1) No person spine: the only universal local identifiers are GEOGRAPHIC -- OCD-division-IDs, which map cleanly to Census FIPS. So a school-board member or councilmember connects to the federal layer only through their district -> FIPS -> place, never through a personal ID. Person-level national IDs exist only inside proprietary vendors (Ballotpedia, BallotReady/CivicEngine), each its own ecosystem, license-restricted, non-redistributable. (2) Money systems are islands: every big-city ethics agency (NYC CFB, LA, SF, Seattle, Philly) mints its own internal filer/committee IDs. The same donor, lobbyist, or politician has a different ID in every city and carries NO FEC ID and NO EIN. A NYC contribution and an FEC contribution from the same person link ONLY by name+address probabilistic match -- and SF/CA redacts street addresses by law, kneecapping even that. (3) The realistic bridges, in order of strength: GEO (OCD-ID <-> FIPS) is STEEL and free -- this is the one solid weld to the federal spine. NAME+ADDRESS entity resolution (donor/lobbyist/official across local + FEC + state) is PROBABILISTIC and the real engineering cost. STATE-LEVEL pre-resolution via FollowTheMoney/OpenSecrets gives you cleaned entity matches but only for state campaigns (current through 2024, now folding into OpenSecrets), not municipal. Bottom line: connect local data to the federal spine through GEOGRAPHY (reliable) and through a NAME-RESOLUTION layer you build yourself (the moat). There is no shortcut ID.
- **Connects to:** Wires in through GEOGRAPHY, not personal IDs. OCD-division-IDs -> Census FIPS is the load-bearing weld: it lets every local official, race, and money flow attach to a district -> county -> the federal members of Congress, USASpending awards, and contracts Ripple already holds. The second connection is a NAME-RESOLUTION / entity-index layer -- and Ripple already has the bones of this: the connect/ module (bridge.py, match.py, resolve.py, entity_index_specs.py, fingerprint.py) is exactly the probabilistic-matching machinery needed to link a NYC/LA/SF donor or lobbyist to an FEC individual donor (now landing per CLAUDE.md) or to a federal entity by name+address. Concretely: land each city's CF/lobbying CSVs into LIBRARY_RAW.LANDING as loc_ sources (loc_nyc_cfb_contributions, loc_sf_ethics_lobbying, etc.), register them in SOURCE_REGISTRY with JOIN_KEYS = [OCD_ID, name, address] and JOIN_KEY_TIER = GEO/PROBABILISTIC, then build int_ models that resolve names against the existing FEC and SAM entity indexes. The federal money spine + this local layer + the connect/ matcher is the whole 'follow one person from school board to Congress' product.
- **Unlocks:** Local-money-meets-federal-money: a developer/donor maxes out to a mayor via NYC CFB AND to that mayor's congressional allies via FEC (already in Ripple) -- name-resolve across both to show the same checkbook buying influence at city hall and on Capitol Hill. The first concrete payoff of the local layer.; Lobbyist-to-contract pipeline at city scale: cross NYC eLobbyist / LA / SF / Philly lobbying registrations against city contracts and (via FIPS) federal USASpending awards -- who lobbied which official, then won the work. 'Councilmember voted on a project they hold stock in' -- pure accountability journalism.; Bundler / intermediary networks: NYC CFB's rare intermediary table and Seattle's Democracy Voucher data expose WHO assembles the money, not just who gives it -- map the fixers behind a campaign.; Banned-but-still-operating, local edition: tie into the memory's flagship lens -- a contractor debarred federally (SAM, already in Ripple) still winning city work, surfaced by name-matching SAM debarments against city vendor/lobbying rolls.; School-board and county-board dark matter: ~519k elected officials almost none of whom are in any federal dataset -- being able to even ENUMERATE them (via Ballotpedia/BallotReady + OCD) and attach local results (OpenElections) is itself a differentiator; layer in culture-war money flowing into school-board races as the 2024-2026 storyline.; Ballot-measure money: who funds local ballot initiatives (housing, policing, taxes) -- the committees behind Prop/Measure X, traceable in NetFile/city CF data, then connected to the same funders' federal and state activity.; Geographic accountability heatmaps: because everything pins to FIPS via OCD-ID, you can render 'influence density' choropleths that fuse local money, federal spending, and federal reps for any county -- the kind of data-viz story the Publishing layer is built for.

### Personal Financial Disclosure & Ethics (all branches): congressional STOCK Act PTRs, annual financial disclosures (House Clerk + Senate eFD), OGE executive-branch 278e, judicial financial disclosures, plus gifts & travel
_The "who's getting rich off public office" layer -- member stock trades, net-worth disclosures, judge/cabinet holdings, and sponsored travel -- all real and mostly free, but locked behind a PDF-parsing wall and a no-shared-ID name-matching problem that is the whole game._

- **Gettability:** moderate · **Verdict:** GET IT -- but phase it, and the value is wildly uneven across the four branches. Tier 1 (do first, high ROI): congressional STOCK Act trades + annual FDs. House is genuinely easy on the index (bulk <YEAR>FD.zip -> XML, FilingType=P for PTRs); Senate needs a DataTables/cookie scraper. Seed the trade history from House/Senate Stock Watcher JSON (verify it's not stale), then run your own pipe for freshness. The real work is two things: (1) PDF transaction-table parsing -- newer PDFs have extractable text, older need OCR, budget a robust parser with a manual-review queue; (2) the name->bioguide crosswalk -- build it ONCE as a reconciliation table and you unlock every insider-trading/conflict story against the spine. That crosswalk IS the project; everything downstream is a join. Tier 2 (high story value, moderate lift): judicial -- DON'T scrape the AO portal, ingest Free Law/CourtListener's already-structured API + CSV bulk (~32k disclosures, ~2M investments). Clean and granular; the catch is it joins to the spine only at the 9 Justices, so you're standing up a judge dimension. Tier 3 (hardest, lowest join payoff): OGE 278e -- legacy Lotus Notes site, no API, no bulk, pure PDF, and appointee filers mostly have no bioguide. High narrative value for the divestiture/cabinet-conflict angle but treat as a targeted-scrape-by-name effort, not bulk ingestion. Gifts/travel: ingest the free House Clerk Gift/Travel filings; cite LegiStorm/Howard Center, don't license them. What it'd take: ~1 robust multi-source scraper (House XML + Senate DataTables + OGE-by-name + Gift/Travel), a hardened PDF/OCR transaction parser with a review queue, the Free Law API integration, and -- the load-bearing piece -- a name-resolution crosswalk table mapping every filer to bioguide/judge_id. That crosswalk is the difference between 'a pile of disclosure PDFs' and 'the live conflict-of-interest engine that no other public platform has wired to the vote/money spine.'
- **Join-key reality:** This is the crux and it is genuinely hard. NONE of these disclosure sources carry bioguide, icpsr, fec_id, CIK, or EIN natively -- they were built by ethics offices, not data people. For CONGRESS (House Clerk + Senate eFD + Stock Watcher), the only handle is filer name + chamber + state(+district). You resolve that to bioguide with a fuzzy match against unitedstates/congress-legislators (the open crosswalk Ripple already effectively has), and once you have bioguide you inherit ALL of Ripple's spine -- votes, committees, FEC money, DW-NOMINATE, bills. The match is ~95% mechanical but the long tail is brutal: maiden/married names, suffixes, mid-term replacements, "Jr.", staff filers who share a surname, and unicode garbage in the PDFs. Budget real reconciliation effort -- this is a build-a-crosswalk-table job, not a one-line join. For the OTHER THREE branches the join largely DOESN'T exist: OGE 278e filers are appointees (no bioguide ever) -- they only touch the spine for the few who were also members of Congress, FARA principals, or named contractor execs (UEI via name, weak). Judicial disclosures join to Ripple's SCDB/Oyez ONLY for the 9 sitting Justices; the other ~16k judges are a brand-new entity universe with no federal-spine ID at all -- you'd be standing up a judge dimension from scratch (Free Law's judge_id becomes your de-facto key). Tickers in the trade data DO join cleanly to securities/CIK-via-SEC, which is a separate, clean bridge worth exploiting. Honest verdict: STEEL join for congressional trades (via the name->bioguide crosswalk you build once), PROBABILISTIC-to-none for executive and judicial.
- **Connects to:** Bolts directly onto the federal spine Ripple already owns. Congressional trades/holdings/travel resolve (via a built-once name->bioguide crosswalk) into the SAME bioguide key as members, votes, committees, cosponsorship, FEC money, and DW-NOMINATE -- meaning a PTR row instantly inherits 'who they are, what they voted on, who funds them, where they sit on the ideology axis.' The executive-branch (OGE) and judicial pieces don't share the spine's IDs and instead EXTEND the entity graph: OGE adds an appointee/cabinet dimension that overlaps the FARA + USASpending-contractor worlds Ripple already has; judicial disclosures stand up a wholly new ~16k-judge dimension that only meets the spine at the 9 Justices (SCDB/Oyez, already in). Net: congressional disclosure is a high-multiplier add to existing nodes; executive/judicial are new branches of the graph.
- **Unlocks:** The flagship insider-trading story: cross-reference each member's STOCK Act PTRs against the bills they sponsored/cosponsored and the committees they sit on (Ripple already has all three keyed to bioguide). 'Senator on the Armed Services Committee bought defense-contractor stock 9 days before a markup' -- fully automatable as a recurring lens, not a one-off.; Trade-vs-vote timing: join PTR transaction dates to roll-call vote dates on related legislation. ### The Lower Judiciary (federal trial/appellate judges, dockets, judicial financial disclosures, judicial ideology, US Sentencing Commission, and state courts)

- **Gettability:** moderate · **Verdict:** GET IT -- the federal tier is a high-value, low-friction win and one of the cleanest extensions of Ripple's existing spine available anywhere on the frontier. Phase 1 (easy, do now): land FJC directory (free CSV), JCS ideology (free CSV), USSC offender datafiles (free), and IDB (free) -- four bulk files, all public-domain or attribution, that immediately give you the judge spine + ideology + sentencing outcomes wired to NOMINATE. Phase 2 (moderate): ingest CourtListener bulk people-db + financial-disclosures CSV from their S3 bucket (public-domain bulk) -- this delivers the crosswalk and the Pulitzer-grade conflict data; the only catch is the full live API went paid-membership in May 2026, but the QUARTERLY BULK CSV is free, so design around the bulk drop, not the API. ### Public Opinion & Surveys (academic survey micro-data + polling aggregators)
_The electorate's actual opinions in microdata form — and the only one that cleanly bolts onto Ripple's federal spine is the Cooperative Election Study, because it ships ~60k respondents/year tagged with congressional district, county FIPS, and validated vote, all under CC0._

- **Gettability:** easy · **Verdict:** GET THE CES NOW — it's the highest-value, lowest-friction frontier source in this whole region: CC0, no auth, one Dataverse download, ~60k respondents/year already tagged with congressional district + county FIPS + validated vote. It is the missing 'voice of the electorate' column that makes Ripple's representation/accountability stories possible, and it joins to the federal spine via geography and named members. Effort: one bulk_file pipeline (download the cumulative common content from Harvard Dataverse -> LANDING -> stage cast -> mart keyed on state+CD and county_fips). Tier-2, also easy and worth grabbing: GSS (open, MIT-permissive codebook, one download) and the FROZEN FiveThirtyEight polls archive (CC-BY on GitHub — snapshot it before it rots; it will not be updated again). Tier-3, account-gated but still bulk: ANES (free account) and Pew ATP (free account) — ingest the public files as a state/region opinion layer; do NOT chase their restricted-use geocodes (ICPSR VDE / NORC contract = enclave-only, no bulk export, not worth it for a public platform). AVOID as ingest targets: Roper/iPoll and live Gallup (paywalled, redistribution-restricted — use as reference lookups, not Library tables) and RealClearPolitics (scrape-only, ToS-gray, aggregate-only — low value once you hold 538). Hard truth to set expectations: surveys never key to a person on the federal spine and never will — respondents are anonymous by design. The connection is GEOGRAPHY and NAMED POLITICIANS, not an ID join. So expect JOIN_KEY_TIER = GEO (STRONG for CES via CD, PROBABILISTIC elsewhere), and budget for MRP (multilevel regression + poststratification) if you want clean district-level opinion estimates from the smaller-n surveys. Bottom line: one easy, genuinely transformative source (CES) + two easy archival grabs (GSS, 538) for maybe a day of pipeline work each.
- **Join-key reality:** This is the crux and it splits the region cleanly in two. WINNER: the Cooperative Election Study is the only source here that hard-wires to Ripple's federal spine. And note the hard wall: survey respondents are anonymous by law/ethics — there is NO person-level ID that ever crosses into the federal spine, and there never can be. The federal spine is about named, accountable entities (members, donors, contractors); surveys are about an anonymized electorate. They meet at GEOGRAPHY (FIPS / CD) and at NAMED POLITICIANS (approval of member X), not at a person key. So join_key_tier here is GEO at best — STRONG only for CES via congressional district, PROBABILISTIC for everything else.
- **Connects to:** CES is the bridge piece: its congressional-district key (state+CD) maps to the member Ripple already holds (bioguide/icpsr crosswalk), and its county_fips maps to Ripple's geographic spine. - **Unlocks:** Representation gap / does my rep vote how my district thinks: join CES district-level opinion (aggregated via MRP) to Ripple's DW-NOMINATE + roll-call votes. ### Dark Money / 527s / Political Nonprofits (IRS 8871/8872, 501(c)(4) political spending, 990s for political orgs, OpenSecrets dark-money tracking)
_A clean, free, EIN-keyed federal layer of money the FEC never sees — IRS 527 filings give you donor-level detail down to name/employer/occupation, and 990s expose the c4 grant-trail — but the donor behind the dark money stays masked by design, and the bridge to the FEC spine is a name-match, not an ID-join._

- **Gettability:** easy · **Verdict:** GREEN LIGHT, and it's near the top of the frontier list. Gettability is EASY for the core: the four highest-value sources (IRS 527 POFD, IRS 990 e-file XML, ProPublica API, IRS EO BMF) are all free, no-auth, bulk/API, clean-licensed, EIN-keyed — exactly the Library's wheelhouse, and four straight onboard.py runs. WHAT IT TAKES: (1) Land the four IRS/ProPublica sources first — POFD (medium: multi-record fixed-layout parse, but the layout file + Pub 5927 hand it to you), EO BMF (easy), ProPublica (easy), then the 990 XML (the real work — versioned-schema parsing; lean on IRSx + the NOPDC concordance rather than rolling your own). (2) Build the EIN self-join subgraph — this is reliable and high-value on its own. (3) Build the EIN<->fec_id probabilistic bridge as an explicit, scored crosswalk table (name+address+treasurer match), and label it PROBABILISTIC in the catalog so nothing downstream treats it as STEEL — this is the one genuinely hard-but-possible piece and the highest leverage. (4) Treat OpenSecrets and FollowTheMoney as benchmark/validation + 'grab the historical archive now' sources, NOT redistributable spines — both carry non-commercial/educational license terms that clash with the commercial ceiling, and FTM is actively being sunset. Honest caveat to set with Chris up front: this region gets you the disclosed-but-FEC-invisible money and the laundering PATH, but it does NOT unmask the original dark-money donor — that layer is masked by design and no source short of litigation/leaks breaks it. The story is the gap and the trail, not the final name.
- **Join-key reality:** EIN is the hero here — and it's a STEEL key WITHIN this region. Every 527 (8871/8872), every c4/c6 990, the EO BMF, and ProPublica all key on EIN. So the political-nonprofit graph self-joins cleanly: 527 donor records -> 990 financials -> org master -> NTEE classification, all on EIN. That alone is a coherent new subgraph.  The brutal truth is the bridge to Ripple's FEC spine. FEC committees DO NOT publicly carry an EIN — fec_committee_id and EIN live in two universes the government never crosswalked. There is no official EIN<->fec_id table. So connecting a dark-money c4 to its super-PAC beneficiary, or a 527 to its FEC-filing twin, is a NAME-AND-ADDRESS MATCH (probabilistic), not an ID join. OpenSecrets/Issue One do this match by hand and with curated internal IDs precisely because the IDs don't line up. Two narrow exceptions where it gets cleaner: (1) federal PACs that are ALSO 527s sometimes appear in both with matchable names/addresses; (2) the DONOR side of an 8872 Schedule A record carries name + employer + occupation — the SAME fields FEC individual contributions carry — so a donor seen giving to a 527 can be probabilistically matched to the individual-donation data landing in Ripple now. And the second hop — the original funder behind a c4 — is masked BY DESIGN; no data source short of litigation or a leak unmasks it. So: STEEL self-join on EIN, PROBABILISTIC bridge to FEC on org name/address, and a hard wall at the true donor.
- **Connects to:** Ripple already holds the FEC money layer (PAC->candidate, outside spending/IEs, and individual donations landing now), every member hard-keyed on bioguide/icpsr/fec_id, and FARA. This region adds the LAYER BELOW the FEC waterline and wires in three ways: (1) EIN becomes a new STEEL spine for a political-nonprofit subgraph (527 + c4 + 990 + EO BMF), which also lights up the existing nonprofit/business graph the memory notes are starving for bridge fuel — this region IS bridge fuel, EIN-native. (2) The probabilistic org-name/address bridge connects c4/527 spenders to the FEC committees already in Ripple, completing the 'dark dollar -> super PAC -> ad against member' chain end-to-end against bioguide-keyed members and their roll-call votes / DW-NOMINATE ideology. (3) The 8872 donor records (name/employer/occupation) join probabilistically to the individual FEC donations now landing, so a donor's disclosed and semi-disclosed giving merge into one footprint. Net: turns Ripple from 'the disclosed federal money' into 'the disclosed money PLUS the money designed to be invisible, with the laundering path drawn.'
- **Unlocks:** Money the FEC never sees: surface the ~$1.9B in 2024 dark-money federal spending (Brennan Center) of which only ~$43M showed up as direct FEC disclosure — quantify the disclosure gap per cycle and per group as a standing metric.; The grant trail (Issue One method, automated): use 990 Schedule I to trace c4 -> c4 -> 527 -> super-PAC money laundering chains that hide the origin, then join the terminal super-PAC to Ripple's FEC outside-spending/IE data to show 'this masked dollar became this attack ad against this member.'; 527 donor unmasking: 8872 Schedule A gives full donor name/employer/occupation for 527s — match those donors (probabilistically) to the individual FEC donations landing in Ripple now to build a 'this donor's TOTAL political footprint' profile spanning disclosed + 527 channels.; Banned-but-operating / revoked-but-spending: cross the EO revocations file against active 8872 filers and 990 political spenders to flag orgs spending politically after losing or while contesting exempt status (ties directly to the 'banned but still operating' first-story lens in memory).; Member-to-darkmoney proximity: a c4 that spent against/for a member (via the super-PAC bridge) whose board members (8871 Type D directors, 990 officers) also show up as the member's donors or former staff — board/officer name graph joined to bioguide-keyed members.; Dual-hat operatives: the same person as 8871 director on multiple 527s AND officer on multiple c4s AND FEC committee treasurer — surface the small network of operatives who run the dark-money machine across entities.; State dark money before it disappears: FollowTheMoney state 527/c4 spending tied to state legislators (no federal ID, but name/geography to ProPublica Congress alumni and revolving-door tracking).

### Deeper Legislative Substance — committee reports & prints, congressional hearings (transcripts/witnesses/testimony), the Congressional Record (floor speeches), amendments, Statements of Administration Policy, scheduled markups

- **Gettability:** easy · **Verdict:** GETTABILITY: easy-to-moderate, and this is the highest leverage-per-effort frontier in the political universe because the keys are already steel. ### Executive Personnel & Oversight (federal) — political appointees, agency leadership, White House visitor logs, federal workforce, and the IG/GAO/congressional/FOIA oversight layer
_A two-halves region: the PERSONNEL half (Plum/PLUM, named federal-employee rosters) is gettable and person-name-keyed — it bridges to FEC/lobbying by NAME, not by a federal ID; the OVERSIGHT half (IG reports, GAO recs, FOIA logs) is gettable and joins cleanly to the AGENCY/contractor spine you already own. No shared person-ID exists, so the value comes from name-resolution and agency joins, not steel keys._

- **Gettability:** moderate · **Verdict:** GO — high value, mostly low-to-medium effort, but pick the winnable fights and be honest about the dark spots. EASY WINS (land first): OPM PLUM register (free JSON/CSV), OPM FWD workforce cubes (free CSV/JSON), FOIA.gov annual-report API (free key), GAO recommendations CSV (gao.gov/open-recs2-csv), and OpenSanctions' pre-resolved PLUM entities (free for non-commercial — but flag the commercial-license risk for the solo/freelance ceiling). MEDIUM: GovInfo API for the quadrennial Plum Book back-catalog (PDF parsing per edition) and GAO/CHRG report text; Oversight.gov IG reports (no central API — scrape node pages, but harvest each OIG's CSV recommendation export). HARDER / EYES-OPEN: the named EHRI federal-employee roster is best gotten by a direct OPM FOIA rather than scraping third-party sites with murky terms; and White House visitor logs are a CLOSED historical corpus (Obama+Biden only) — the current administration releases nothing until 5 years post-term, so treat it as archive + FOIA, not a live feed. THE REAL WORK isn't ingestion, it's the person-name resolution layer: build an appointee/person bridge (seeded by OpenSanctions IDs) so PLUM names match into FEC/FARA/LDA — that's a few days of fuzzy-matching engineering and the thing that turns this region from a pile of rosters into the revolving-door engine. Estimate: ~1 day each for the 5 easy API/CSV sources; ~2-3 days for the person-bridge; Oversight.gov scraping is the only genuinely annoying ingest.
- **Join-key reality:** Brutal truth: this region splits into two join realities. (1) PERSONNEL = PERSON-NAME-KEYED, NO STEEL ID. Plum/PLUM, the named EHRI roster, and visitor logs all identify people by name + agency + title only — there is no bioguide/FEC-ID/EIN on a bureaucrat or appointee. So connecting an appointee to their FEC donations, lobbying registrations, or board seats is a PROBABILISTIC name-match (name + employer + city), not a key join. OpenSanctions' pre-resolved PLUM entity IDs are the single biggest shortcut here — they hand you a stable person ID and PEP cross-refs you'd otherwise hand-build. (2) OVERSIGHT = AGENCY-KEYED, CLEAN. IG reports, GAO recs, and FOIA metrics all carry agency/component names that join straight to the agency dimension already implied by your USASpending contracts, Federal Register, and the Plum/FWD agency lists. Within oversight, GAO's CSV even carries Publication Number (rec->report is a real key). And the appointee NAME is the bridge that lets you cross the two halves AND reach your existing FEC/FARA spine — e.g. take an appointee from PLUM, name-match into FEC individual donations (which you said are landing now) and FARA principals. So: no new steel key enters the Library here, but you gain (a) a clean agency join into oversight, and (b) a person-name bridge that — once resolved — wires appointees to the money spine you already own.
- **Connects to:** Wires into the federal spine on two seams. SEAM 1 (clean, agency): IG reports + GAO recommendations + FOIA metrics all key on agency/component, which joins directly to your USASpending contracts, SAM debarments, Federal Register/EO issuers, and the Plum/FWD agency lists — instant 'oversight pressure vs. money/power per agency' marts. SEAM 2 (probabilistic, person-name): PLUM/Plum-Book appointees and the named EHRI roster are the missing 'who runs the agencies' layer that your spine lacks — and the appointee NAME is the bridge to FEC individual donations (landing now), FARA principals, and planned LDA lobbying. This is exactly the kind of bridge your MEMORY notes flag as starved: there's no shared steel ID, so you'd build a person-resolution table (name + agency + locale, seeded by OpenSanctions' PLUM entity IDs) as a new bridge artifact — the same pattern as the CCN<->NPI crosswalk, applied to people. Net: oversight half drops in as agency-keyed marts; personnel half adds a new person-name bridge that finally connects 'who holds power' to 'who pays for it.'
- **Unlocks:** Revolving door, quantified: name-match PLUM/Plum-Book appointees against FARA principals, federal lobbying (LDA, already planned), and FEC individual donations (landing now) — 'this Schedule C appointee at Agency X lobbied that same agency 18 months earlier / donated $X to the confirming senators.'; Appointee-to-money: cross PLUM names with FEC donor records to surface political appointees who bundled or gave to the administration that hired them — a clean, repeatable 'donors who got jobs' story every transition.; Vacancy/ghost-government map: PLUM's vacant-vs-filled flag + the GAO-documented missing 130+ PAS roles = 'how much of the executive branch is running on actings and empties,' refreshable on every republish.; Oversight heat map: join Oversight.gov IG findings + GAO open recommendations to USASpending contract dollars per agency — 'the agencies with the most unaddressed watchdog findings are moving the most contract money,' and rank by oldest-unimplemented priority recs.; Recommendation graveyard: GAO/IG open recs carry issue dates and status — track which agencies sit on critical recommendations for years, with the dollar-savings GAO/CIGIE attach to each.; Who's in the room: the Obama+Biden visitor-log corpus name-matched to lobbyists (LDA) and FARA agents — 'registered foreign agent X visited the NSC Y times,' a finished ProPublica-style story you can rebuild and extend, with the honest caveat that the current administration has gone dark.; FOIA stonewalling index: FOIA.gov annual-report metrics rank agencies by backlog growth, denial/exemption rates, and response time — 'the agencies hiding the most are also the ones under the most IG scrutiny,' joinable to the oversight layer by agency.; Workforce-vs-mission: OPM FWD headcount/occupation trends per agency against contract spend and IG findings — 'this agency's workforce shrank 30% while its contract obligations doubled and its IG backlog tripled.'

### Politician communications (say-vs-do corpus) + frontier edges: territories/DC, tribal governments, and protest/civic-mobilization event data
_The communications cluster is a goldmine that snaps onto the federal spine on bioguide_id (tweets, Bluesky, press releases, e-newsletters, floor speeches = a clean say-vs-do corpus); the frontier edges (protest data, territories, tribal) are real and valuable but join via geography (FIPS/lat-lon) or not at all -- there is no shared person-level ID linking a Guam senator or a tribal council to anything Ripple holds._

- **Gettability:** moderate · **Verdict:** GET THE COMMUNICATIONS CLUSTER NOW -- it's the highest value-to-effort ratio in the entire frontier. congress-press (bioguide baked in, MIT, daily) and congresstweets (MIT, archive-backed) are near-trivial bulk-file onboards. GovInfo CREC for floor speeches is a free-key API but parsing-heavy (budget real effort). DCinbox is easy to pull but single-maintainer -- mirror it and resolve names to bioguide. Together these four complete the say-vs-do corpus, and the join to Ripple's spine is STEEL, not probabilistic. Effort: ~1-2 weeks for the clean three, plus a CREC parsing project.\n\nBUILD BLUESKY AS A MOAT -- but know it's net-new construction: no bioguide<->Bluesky crosswalk exists, so you create and own it (starter-pack scrape -> handle->DID -> hand-match to bioguide). Bluesky's open firehose makes the ongoing pull cheap, unlike X. Medium-hard, high uniqueness.\n\nTAKE CCC FREE for protest data; it's well-licensed (Harvard Dataverse, FIPS-ready, weekly) and joins by geography. HOLD ACLED until the noncommercial-license question is answered -- it's a genuine landmine for a monetized platform; use it only as enrichment/validation, not the primary, and get the license sorted before publishing anything off it.\n\nTREAT THE EDGES AS A SEPARATE, LOWER-PRIORITY TRACK. DC OCF is a clean bulk download and worth grabbing. Tribal (BIA directory) is an easy entity table but only name-matches to money. Territories beyond DC/PR are PDF-scrape slogs with thin payoff and NO federal join key -- pursue only when a specific story demands it, and prefer mirroring The Accountability Project's aggregation over scraping each portal. Be honest in the catalog: tag these GEO or PROBABILISTIC, never STEEL.
- **Join-key reality:** Two completely different stories, and you must not conflate them.  THE COMMUNICATIONS CLUSTER = STEEL JOIN. Tweets, press releases, e-newsletters, floor speeches, and (with effort) Bluesky all resolve to bioguide_id -- the exact key Ripple's entire federal spine already turns on. congress-press literally nests bioguide_id in every record. congresstweets needs one crosswalk hop (account -> member via the automator's user file -> bioguide via congress-legislators). GovInfo CREC carries speaker metadata in the granule XML. This cluster snaps on with zero probabilistic matching. It is the cleanest new data in the whole frontier.  THE ONE BLUESKY CAVEAT: the bioguide<->Bluesky map does NOT exist in any maintained file (congress-legislators tracks twitter+mastodon, not bluesky). You build that crosswalk yourself -- which is precisely why it's a moat.  THE PROTEST CLUSTER = GEO JOIN ONLY. CCC and ACLED carry FIPS/lat-lon, not people. You can answer 'how many protests hit district X' or 'which issues mobilized in counties a member represents,' joining through the district->county geography Ripple can derive. You CANNOT join a protest to a politician by any shared ID. That's fine -- geographic is the honest, correct grain for event data -- but know it's GEO tier, not STEEL.  THE EDGES (territories, DC, tribal) = LARGELY NO JOIN. This is the brutal-honesty part. A DC Council member, a Puerto Rico legislator, a tribal council chair -- none carry a bioguide, FEC ID, ICPSR, or UEI. The only threads to the federal spine are: (1) the non-voting territorial DELEGATES / Resident Commissioner, who ARE keyed in Ripple's Congress data; (2) tribes and territories appearing as NAME-matched lobbying clients / FEC contributors / FARA principals -- fuzzy string joins, PROBABILISTIC tier at best. Treat these edges as standalone local-accountability datasets that occasionally name-match into the money layer, not as keyed extensions of the federal graph.
- **Connects to:** Ripple's federal spine is keyed on bioguide/icpsr/fec_id/UEI and already holds members, votes, bills, cosponsors, committees, FEC money, FARA, and the Federal Register. This region's communications cluster bolts straight onto bioguide_id: it adds the MISSING 'what they say' dimension to complete the say-vs-do graph against the 'what they do' Ripple already has (votes + money). Floor speeches (CREC) sit adjacent to roll-call votes; press releases + e-newsletters + tweets are member-level text layers indexed by the same member key. Protest data joins through geography -- Ripple can derive district->county FIPS, and CCC's fips_code makes that a clean merge, layering civic-mobilization context onto the district level (and onto Census, which Ripple touches). The org/actor names in CCC/ACLED and the tribe/territory names name-match (fuzzily) into the lobbying + FEC contributor tables Ripple is building. The territorial delegates are the one hard ID bridge from the edges back into the keyed Congress data.
- **Unlocks:** SAY vs DO, fully wired: join floor-speech text + press releases + tweets + e-newsletters (what a member SAYS) against roll-call votes + cosponsorships + FEC money Ripple already holds (what they DO). Surface members who rail against an industry on the floor while taking its PAC money and voting its way -- the single highest-value story this whole region unlocks, and it's a clean bioguide join.; Deleted-tweet accountability: the congresstweets archive captures posts before members delete them. Cross-reference deletions against votes, scandals, and market-moving events -- 'what they walked back' is a story only an archive can tell.; Message discipline / astroturf detection: cluster press releases and e-newsletters by text similarity to expose copy-paste talking points distributed across members (often traceable to a leadership office, PAC, or lobbying shop named in LDA/FARA data Ripple is planning).; Issue attention vs constituent reality: bigram/topic-model what a member talks about (DCinbox + CREC) against what's actually happening in their district -- e.g. silent on a major local protest wave (CCC FIPS join) or on local economic distress.; Protest pressure -> legislative response: time-series CCC protest events in a district against that member's subsequent floor speeches, bill introductions, and votes. Does mobilization move the representative, or not? Geographic join, real causal-adjacent narrative.; Platform migration as a tell: who left X for Bluesky, when, and how their rhetoric changed across platforms (open Bluesky firehose vs the X archive) -- a media/polarization story with a built-in natural experiment.; The territories' representation gap: contrast the local campaign-finance ecosystems (DC OCF, PR OCE) and the lived politics of 4M+ disenfranchised Americans against their single non-voting delegate's federal floor speeches and (losing) votes -- a structural-injustice series almost nobody covers with data.; Tribal sovereignty money trail: tribe (BIA directory) -> federal lobbying client (OpenSecrets W09 / LDA) -> contributions -> the members on the committees with jurisdiction (Indian Affairs) -- following gaming, land, and water fights, stitched by name-match into the money layer.
